Spurs are eating at the top table because they make 800k per match in their restaurants alone

Publish date: 2024-02-06

THEY are having the rest of the Premier League for breakfast, lunch and dinner.

Tottenham, four wins on the spin at their glamorous new home, are serving up something special off the field.

Spurs are generating a staggering £800,000 in revenue at each home game with an endless choice of suites, restaurants, bars and street-food outlets on ­stadium concourses.

Based on 19 Premier League games and a minimum of three Champions League group matches, that is an additional income of £17.6million.

Revenue like that pays for Harry Kane’s yearly salary.

To put those sales into perspective, elsewhere Premier League leaders Manchester City admit they do well to bring in £150,000 a game from hospitality at the Etihad.

Tottenham’s approach is making the rest of the Premier League work out what they can do to bridge the gap.

Premium London prices aside, City know Tottenham’s limitless range of experiences eclipse anything else on offer at a European football stadium.

The big bucks are spent in fine dining halls, such as The Vault, the Chef’s Table or Galvin’s.

Mid-market offerings, such as the various street-food vendors, the club’s own micro brewery serving craft ales, plus the longest bar in football and their three-second-per-pint beer pumps, swell the coffers further.

'HAVING A DISCO'

Next season the air bridge, suspended from the roof of the North Stand, will double up as a viewing platform and nightclub. Tottenham are having a disco.

The bright lights are on them because this 62,000-capacity stadium has already turned into a giant money-making venture.

When NFL, plus rugby and other corporate events such as concerts, take place, they will skim off millions more from catering sales.

It has forced rival Premier League clubs to think again, to review stadium strategy and to consider other ways to bring in extra revenue.

Arsenal’s posh seating areas and spacious dining halls were avant-garde when the Emirates threw open its doors in 2006.

Already they look tired and dated.

There will be a refurb this summer, with Arsenal borrowing a few ideas from just down the road to bring in some more cash.

They are preparing to kit out some of the ageing restaurants to cope with modern tastes. It is progress or regress.

'THE NEXT LEVEL'

City have started a consultation process to work on another facelift for the Etihad.

They were regarded as innovators when the swanky tunnel club opened at the start of last season.

As you might expect, City had a good poke around the new Tottenham stadium when they went down for the Champions League quarter-final.

They realised Spurs have taken ­hospitality to the next level, with the perfect blend for fans of all ages and incomes.

Across the road at United, the money men at Old Trafford are still waiting for their first trip to the new stadium.

When they get there, they will realise that a leaky roof and the 1970s-style storage heaters in the corridors at the Theatre of Dreams, belong in the past.

They had all the mod-cons then, with executive boxes and pre-match dining catering for wealthy fans.

Now the pokey lounges, with their low ceilings and tight spaces, look like they are from the miserable  Britannia Hotel on Portland Street.

United still bring in the coin, with more than £590m passing through the tills last year.

But there is also an acceptance the stadium needs some kind of upgrade for the 76,000 punters who need something to take their mind off the football at the minute.

United are falling short of their own standards, left behind by the wise heads 220 miles down the road in London.

With an extra £800,000 coming in every home game, Spurs are dining at the top table.
